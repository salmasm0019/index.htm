Loneliness kills like smoking — and Ireland's hidden victims are 35–64, not 65+

Irish policy targets older adults. OECD data from 32 countries shows the highest loneliness in Ireland is among working-age adults. The intervention is aimed at the wrong group.

4.47% of Ireland's 35–64 adults feel lonely — higher than any other Irish age group
+21% rise in middle-aged loneliness in Ireland between the two OECD measurement points
9.45% of Irish adults lack any social support — nearly 1 in 10 with nobody to turn to
Addressed to: Prof. Rose Anne Kenny, Principal Investigator, TILDA (Irish Longitudinal Study on Ageing)  ·  Action: commission TILDA analysis on working-age isolation and pilot intervention for adults aged 35–64
Main finding · OECD data · 32 countries
Ireland's loneliest adults are 35–64, not 65+. Policy is targeting the wrong group.
In most countries, loneliness peaks in old age. Ireland breaks this pattern. Middle-aged adults report higher loneliness than older adults — a finding with no equivalent in current Irish policy. Switch age groups below. Ireland is in red.

One recommended action for Prof. Rose Anne Kenny, TILDA

TILDA is Ireland's most authoritative longitudinal study — but its scope begins at age 50. The data above shows the crisis begins at 35. Commissioning a TILDA analysis of working-age isolation (35–64) would be the first Ireland-specific evidence base for this group — and would give the next Commission on Loneliness the data it needs to recommend a targeted pilot intervention.

Recommended action: Commission TILDA analysis on working-age isolation, ages 35–64
